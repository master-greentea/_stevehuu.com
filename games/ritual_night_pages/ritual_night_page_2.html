Our Design Process

With a small core team of 4, all of us participated in designing the main gameplay to some degree. Out of us 4, Mike (Creative Director) and I acted as the Lead Designers most of the time, and together, we created the following process that we have been using:

  • Ideation: One of us would pitch something (usually with a document or a deck).
  • Discussion: Everyone sits (or stands) in front of a whiteboard and talk about how the design fits into the existing game. This meeting is usually led by whoever brought up the idea.
  • Action items: At the end of discussion, I breakdown everything into a comprehensible list of action items, and assign them to relevant team members.
  • Implementation & Discord feedback: Once whoever implementing or prototyping is done, they will create a thread in our discord with a template structure:
    • What was done?
    • Feedback questions
    • Directed feedback questions (@'ing a specific team member)
    • Further actions
  • Weekly playtest sessions: We playtest our game every week regardless of whether or not there's new content. This is followed by another quick discussion similar to the discord feedback where the designers ask specific questions to testers.
  • Iterate, repeat, and polish: Repeat all the steps above until we end up with something we are happy with (Usually never).

Tutorialization

The problem with a team of game design student is that how do we teach our extravagantly over designed game to normal people. Thus tutorialization is crucial. We need to find the best way to progressively familiarize the players with Ritual Night.

Design Goal

My goals for tutorialization is simple:

Get new players into a game armed with as much info as they need to get started, as fast as possible.

I came up with the following principals:

  • How to do? > What to do? (teach buttons, not strategies)
  • Letting players do > Telling players what to do
  • Do not overload information, break into digestible chunks

The Result

I separated the tutorialization into 3 parts:

Active

Active tutorials are there to teach the players the basics by explaining what they are and immediately letting them practice it.

Passive

Players learn as they play. If they do it once in-game, it's far more effective then explaining to them 10 times in isolation. Passive tutorials allow players to learn naturally as they play.

These elements should not overshadow actual gameplay. It also isn't 100% necessary information to get you started. I made sure players can easily choose to ignore them or turn them off.
