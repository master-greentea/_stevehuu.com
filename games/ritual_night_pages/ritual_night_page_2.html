Our Design Process

As a small core design team of 4, all of us participated in designing the core gameplay to some degree. Out of us 4, Mike (Creative Director) and I acted as the Lead Designers most of the time, and together, we created the following process that we have been using:

  • Ideation
    One of us would pitch something usually with a document or a deck.
  • Discussion
    Everyone sits down (or stands up when it gets exciting) in front of the whiteboard and talk about how the design fits into the existing game. This meeting is usually led by whoever brought up the idea.
  • Action items
    At the end of discussion, either Mike or I will breakdown everything in the meeting and into a comprehensible list of action items, and assign to relevant team members.
  • Implementation & Discord feedback
    Once whoever implementing or prototyping is done, they will create a thread in our discord with this structure that I templated:
    • What was done?
    • Feedback questions
    • Directed feedback questions (@’ing a specific team member)
    • Further actions
  • Weekly playtest sessions
    We playtest our game every week regardless of whether or not there’s new content. This is usually followed by another quick discussion structured similar to the discord feedback where the designers ask specific questions to the testers.
  • Iterate, repeat, and polish
    Repeat all the steps above until we end up with something we are happy with. (Usually never, since everything gets iterated perpetually after every playtest…)

Tutorialization

I was given full control of one of the major aspect of the game: Tutorialization.

The Problem

As a team entirely made of game design students, everyone has good ideas. Right from our first few playtests with the original prototype, we quickly realized the game is much more complicated than its usual competitors on the market. Thus tutorialization is crucial. We need to find the best way to progressively familiarize the players with Ritual Night.

My goals for tutorialization is simple:

Get new players to a point where - they can start playing the game with some confusion, but not enough to cause frustration - as fast and entertaining as possible.

To achieve this goal I came up with the following principals:

  • How to do? > What to do? (or… teach the buttons, not the strategies)
  • Letting players do > Telling players what to do
  • Do not feed too much information at once, break into digestible chunks

The Hurdle

At first, I started with prototyping a full-on guided tutorial like many others. It was able to convey the core idea, however very quickly I became aware of its problem: it’s boring and it shoves too much important information in your too quickly.

The Solution

To fix it I separated the tutorialization into 2 parts:

Active

Active tutorials are there to teach the players the very basics by both directly explaining what they are, and immediately letting them practice it.

Passive

Players usually want to learn as they play. If they done it once in-game, it’s far more effective then explaining to them 10 times in isolation. Thus I put more effort into passive tutorials that players would get naturally as they play.

These elements should not overshadow actual gameplay, it is ok for players to ignore them or turn them off, but still be present for those who need them.
